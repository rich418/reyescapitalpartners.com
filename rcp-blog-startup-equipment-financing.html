Equipment Financing for Startups in California: What Banks Won't Tell You

Under 2 years in business? Here's how to get approved fast — even when your bank says no.

By Oscar Reyes · Reyes Capital Partners Ventura, CA 6 min read

You started your business. You have the clients, the contracts, the crew — but not the equipment. And every bank you've talked to has the same answer: come back when you've been in business two years.

That's the wall most startup contractors, truckers, and small operators hit in California. The bank's minimum time-in-business requirement isn't about whether you can make a payment — it's about how their risk models work. You're not a bad borrower. You just don't fit their box yet.

The good news: the bank isn't your only option, and it's not even your best option when you're starting out.

"Most lenders decline startups because of policy, not ability to pay. The right lender looks at your contracts, your cash flow, and the equipment itself — not just how long you've had an EIN."

Why Banks Say No to Startups

Traditional banks and credit unions have rigid underwriting criteria built for established businesses. Most require:

  • 2+ years of business tax returns
  • 650–720+ personal FICO score
  • Minimum annual revenue ($250K+)
  • Clean business credit history

If you're in your first or second year, you simply can't meet those criteria — not because you're risky, but because you haven't had time to build the paper trail banks require. A contractor who just landed a $400K grading contract is a good borrower. A bank's model doesn't know that yet.

How Startup Equipment Financing Actually Works

Specialty lenders — not banks — underwrite startup equipment deals differently. Instead of relying solely on business history, they look at:

The Equipment Itself

A 2022 excavator holds its value. If you stop making payments, the lender can recover the asset. That collateral changes the risk equation dramatically — it's why you can get a $75,000 equipment loan with 6 months in business when you couldn't get a $20,000 unsecured business loan.

Your Personal Credit Profile

Most startup lenders look at your personal FICO. 600+ is workable with many lenders. 580+ with the right story and equipment can still get done. It's not "any credit, no questions" — but it's a far wider window than traditional banks allow.

Your Industry and Experience

Ten years in construction as an employee before starting your own company counts. Lenders who work with contractors understand that business age and industry experience are different things. Your track record matters, even before it shows up on a tax return.

What You Need to Apply

For deals up to $75,000 — which covers most contractor equipment purchases — many lenders only need a one-page application. No tax returns, no financials, no business plan. Specifically:

  • Completed credit application (one page)
  • Equipment details: make, model, year, price, and seller
  • Driver's license
  • Voided business check

For larger transactions ($75K–$250K), expect to provide 3–6 months of bank statements and sometimes a year of personal tax returns. Still far less paperwork than a bank loan.

Rates and Terms: What to Expect

Startup financing costs more than prime-credit financing — that's the honest reality. Where an established business with strong credit might get 6–8%, a startup might see 12–22% depending on the lender, equipment type, and credit profile.

Here's the right way to think about it: if a $60,000 piece of equipment lets you take on $200,000 in new contracts this year, the financing cost is a rounding error. The equipment pays for itself. The alternative — waiting two years until you "qualify" — costs far more in lost revenue than any interest rate.

Section 179 of the tax code lets you deduct the full cost of financed equipment in the year you purchase it — up to $1.22 million in 2026. Even a startup can use this. Talk to your CPA.

Common Mistakes Startup Borrowers Make

  1. Applying to banks first and getting declined. Hard inquiries from bank declines can drop your score 5–15 points each. Come to a specialist first.
  2. Not knowing the equipment details. Lenders need year, make, model, hours, and seller. Have this ready before you call.
  3. Waiting for "better credit." If the equipment generates revenue, get it now. Time in business and payment history built on this loan will make your next financing cheaper.
  4. Working with a broker who doesn't know startup lending. Many brokers only work prime credit. Make sure whoever you work with has actual lender relationships in the startup/challenged-credit space.

The Bottom Line

Being a startup in California doesn't disqualify you from equipment financing — it just means you need the right lender. Banks aren't the right lender for year one. Specialty lenders who understand contractors, truckers, and small operators are.
