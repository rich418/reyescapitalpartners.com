Section 179 and Equipment Financing in 2026: The Write-Off Most California Contractors Miss

You can deduct the full cost of financed equipment this year. You don't have to pay cash to get the tax benefit.

By Oscar Reyes · Reyes Capital Partners Ventura, CA 6 min read

Most contractors know Section 179 exists. Far fewer know that you can use Section 179 on financed equipment — you don't have to write a check for the full purchase price to get the deduction.

That single fact changes the math on equipment decisions significantly. Here's how it works, what the 2026 limits are, and why financing is often the smarter play even when you have the cash to buy outright.

Section 179 is a federal tax provision — not a California-specific program. This article covers the federal deduction. California has its own conformity rules that differ; always confirm with a CPA licensed in California before making decisions based on this.

What Section 179 Actually Is

Section 179 of the IRS tax code allows businesses to deduct the full purchase price of qualifying equipment in the year it's placed in service — rather than depreciating it over 5, 7, or 10 years under normal depreciation schedules.

In plain terms: you buy (or finance) an excavator in 2026, put it to work in 2026, and deduct the full cost on your 2026 federal tax return instead of spreading it across years.

The 2026 deduction limit is $1,220,000 — meaning most contractor equipment purchases fall well within the cap.

Why Financing Doesn't Disqualify You

This is the part most people get wrong. Section 179 allows you to deduct the full equipment cost even if you financed it. The IRS cares that the equipment is placed in service and used for business — not how you paid for it.

The practical implication: you can finance $70,000 worth of equipment, make monthly payments of roughly $1,400–1,800/month, and still deduct the full $70,000 on your taxes in year one. You get the tax benefit immediately while preserving your cash for operations.

What Qualifies for Section 179 in 2026

Most business equipment qualifies. For contractors and operators specifically:

  • Excavators, loaders, dozers, graders
  • Skid steers and compact track loaders
  • Commercial trucks and trailers
  • Dump trucks, flatbeds, equipment haulers
  • Concrete equipment, pumps, mixers
  • Landscaping and agricultural equipment
  • Forklifts and material handling equipment
  • Certain software used for business

The equipment must be used more than 50% for business purposes and must be placed in service — actually put to work — before December 31, 2026.

The California Wrinkle

California does not fully conform to federal Section 179 rules. The state has its own deduction limit, which has historically been lower than the federal cap. For 2026, California's conformity status should be confirmed with a CPA — you may not get the same deduction on your state return that you get on your federal return.

This doesn't eliminate the benefit — it just means your federal and state returns will handle the deduction differently. The federal savings are real and significant; the state treatment is a separate calculation.

The bottom line on California: get the federal deduction — it's substantial. Then let your CPA handle the state side. Don't let uncertainty about California conformity stop you from capturing the federal write-off.

How This Changes the Financing Conversation

When a contractor considers financing equipment, the rate conversation usually dominates. Section 179 reframes that conversation. If you're in a 22–24% tax bracket and financing $80,000 in equipment, the federal tax savings of $17,600–$19,200 effectively reduces your financing cost significantly — before a single payment is made.

That's why the "I'll wait until I have better credit for a lower rate" calculation often doesn't hold up. A higher rate today plus a Section 179 deduction this year frequently beats a lower rate next year with no deduction. Do the math with your CPA, but don't assume waiting is the financially conservative choice.

Who This Strategy Works Best For

  • Profitable businesses with a real tax bill to offset
  • Contractors who need equipment now but want to preserve cash
  • Businesses planning Q4 equipment purchases anyway — move earlier to ensure placement before year-end
  • Business owners with challenged credit who have been waiting — the deduction doesn't require prime credit

It works less well for businesses that aren't profitable yet, since a deduction only helps if you have taxable income to offset.

The Bottom Line

Section 179 in 2026 is real, it applies to financed equipment, and most California contractors are leaving money on the table by not factoring it into their equipment decisions. The deduction limit is $1.22 million — more than enough to cover any single purchase most operators would consider.
